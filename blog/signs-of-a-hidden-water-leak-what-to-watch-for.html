Signs of a Hidden Water Leak: What Hill Country Homeowners Should Watch For

Not every leak announces itself with a puddle on the floor. Hidden leaks under slabs, behind walls, or under landscaping can run for weeks—or months—before you notice. By then you may be paying for wasted water, facing foundation risk, or dealing with mold. Catching the early signs saves money and protects the house.

Common Warning Signs

  • Unexplained high water bills: Usage that jumps without a change in household habits is one of the clearest red flags.
  • Meter still spinning with everything off: Shut off all fixtures and appliances, then watch the meter. Movement often means water is escaping somewhere.
  • Damp spots, soft drywall, or peeling paint: Moisture that keeps returning in the same place can mean a supply or drain line behind the wall.
  • Musty odors or mold in closed areas: Persistent smell in closets, cabinets, or crawl spaces often points to hidden moisture.
  • Warm spots on floors or slabs: A hot-water line leak under a slab can warm a patch of flooring even when HVAC is off.
  • Low water pressure in one area: A partial blockage or leak on a branch line can reduce flow to a bathroom or kitchen.
  • Running toilets or hissing near fixtures: Small fixture leaks add up fast and are easy to miss if you are not listening for them.
  • Soggy yard patches or unusually green strips: Outdoor supply leaks can feed lawn growth in one line while the rest of the yard stays dry.

Why Leaks Hide in Hill Country Homes

Many Kerrville-area homes sit on slabs, use private wells or older municipal service lines, and deal with hard water that stresses fittings and heaters. Drought-to-rain cycles and rocky soils can also shift lines over time. That mix makes slab leaks, irrigation tie-ins, and aging water heaters common culprits.

What You Can Check Safely

  1. Compare bills month to month and note spikes that do not match guests, pool fills, or irrigation changes.
  2. Do a meter test with all water off for 15–30 minutes.
  3. Listen near water heaters, toilets, and under sinks for hissing or intermittent refill sounds.
  4. Look under cabinets and around the water heater for corrosion, mineral trails, or damp insulation.

Do not open walls or cut into slabs yourself. If the meter moves with everything off, or you see structural moisture, call a licensed plumber for leak detection.

When to Call a Professional

Call sooner rather than later if you have recurring damp spots, a slab warm zone, sudden pressure loss, or a meter that will not stop. Pros can use acoustic listening, moisture meters, and camera inspection to locate the leak with less guesswork—and less unnecessary demolition.

Stopping Small Leaks Early

Replace failing supply lines, service aging water heaters, and address hard-water scale before fittings fail. A quick inspection before buying a home—or after a big bill spike—often costs far less than repair after a long-running leak.
